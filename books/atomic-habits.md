# Atomic Habits by James Clear

## Page 1–20

- A habit is a routine or behavior performed regularly and automatically  
- Habits are formed to solve recurring problems efficiently  
- The four-step habit loop consists of **cue, craving, response, and reward**  
- Improving by 1% each day results in being ~37 times better in one year  
- Transformation is gradual, not instant  
- Breakthroughs are the result of many small improvements compounding over time  

## Page 21–40

- The **Plateau of Latent Potential** explains why progress often feels invisible at first  
- Habits must persist long enough to produce visible results  
- Mastery requires patience and consistency  
- Every habit begins with a small decision  
- Success requires setting **specific and actionable goals**  
- Goals define direction; systems create progress  
- Winners and losers often share the same goals  
- Achieving a goal creates only temporary change  
- Goals can limit happiness and conflict with long-term growth  
- You do not rise to the level of your goals; you fall to the level of your systems  
- Two habit-change mistakes: changing the wrong thing or changing the wrong way  
- Three layers of behavior change:
  - **Outcome** – what you get  
  - **Process** – what you do  
  - **Identity** – what you believe  
- Habits are expressions of identity  
- When pride is involved, habits are fiercely protected  
- Habit change follows a two-step process:
  1. Decide who you want to become  
  2. Prove it with small wins  

## Page 41–60

- Habits are the simplest solutions to repeated environmental problems  
- Habits function as mental shortcuts learned through experience  
- Good habits increase freedom over time  
- Habit formation follows the same four-stage loop:
  - Cue initiates behavior  
  - Craving motivates behavior  
  - Response is the action  
  - Reward reinforces repetition  
- Without cue, craving, and response, behavior does not occur  
- Without reward, behavior does not repeat  
- **Four Laws of Behavior Change**:
  - Make it obvious  
  - Make it attractive  
  - Make it easy  
  - Make it satisfying  
- **Inverse Laws for breaking bad habits**:
  - Make it invisible  
  - Make it unattractive  
  - Make it difficult  
  - Make it unsatisfying  
- The brain constantly scans the environment for cues  

## Page 61–80

- Behavior change begins with awareness  
- The **Pointing-and-Calling** method increases conscious awareness of habits  
- The **Habit Scorecard** identifies and evaluates current habits  
- **Implementation intentions** specify when and where habits will occur  
- Clear plans reduce decision fatigue and increase follow-through  
- **Habit stacking** links new habits to existing ones  
- Habits can compound positively or negatively when stacked  

## Page 81–100

- People choose actions based on their current context, not future goals  
- Behavior is heavily influenced by environmental cues  
- Behavior is a function of the person and their environment  
- **Suggestion impulse buying** occurs when visual cues trigger imagined needs  
- Environment design can make good habits obvious and easy  
- Habits are associated with specific locations  
- New environments make habit change easier due to lack of existing cues  
- Disciplined people reduce exposure to temptation  
- It is better to avoid temptation than rely on self-control  
- **Cue-induced wanting** triggers cravings for bad habits  
- Self-control is a short-term and unreliable strategy  

## Page 101–120

- The most effective habits are easy to start and hard to stop  
- Reduce friction for good habits and increase friction for bad ones  
- The **Two-Minute Rule** states that new habits should take less than two minutes  
- Habits should be scaled down to their simplest form  
- Standardize before optimizing  
- Motion creates the illusion of progress, but action produces results  
- Repetition, not perfection, builds mastery  
- Consistency matters more than intensity  
- Small habits establish the foundation for larger behaviors  
- Showing up consistently reinforces identity
