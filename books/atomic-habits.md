# Atomic Habits by James Clear

## Page 1–20

- A habit is a routine or behavior performed regularly and automatically  
- Habits are formed to solve recurring problems efficiently  
- The four-step habit loop consists of **cue, craving, response, and reward**  
- Improving by 1% each day results in being ~37 times better in one year  
- Transformation is gradual, not instant  
- Breakthroughs are the result of many small improvements compounding over time  

## Page 21–40

- The **Plateau of Latent Potential** explains why progress often feels invisible at first  
- Habits must persist long enough to produce visible results  
- Mastery requires patience and consistency  
- Every habit begins with a small decision  
- Success requires setting **specific and actionable goals**  
- Goals define direction; systems create progress  
- Winners and losers often share the same goals  
- Achieving a goal creates only temporary change  
- Goals can limit happiness and conflict with long-term growth  
- You do not rise to the level of your goals; you fall to the level of your systems  
- Two habit-change mistakes: changing the wrong thing or changing the wrong way  
- Three layers of behavior change:
  - **Outcome** – what you get  
  - **Process** – what you do  
  - **Identity** – what you believe  
- Habits are expressions of identity  
- When pride is involved, habits are fiercely protected  
- Habit change follows a two-step process:
  1. Decide who you want to become  
  2. Prove it with small wins  

## Page 41–60

- Habits are the simplest solutions to repeated environmental problems  
- Habits function as mental shortcuts learned through experience  
- Good habits increase freedom over time  
- Habit formation follows the same four-stage loop:
  - Cue initiates behavior  
  - Craving motivates behavior  
  - Response is the action  
  - Reward reinforces repetition  
- Without cue, craving, and response, behavior does not occur  
- Without reward, behavior does not repeat  
- **Four Laws of Behavior Change**:
  - Make it obvious  
  - Make it attractive  
  - Make it easy  
  - Make it satisfying  
- **Inverse Laws for breaking bad habits**:
  - Make it invisible  
  - Make it unattractive  
  - Make it difficult  
  - Make it unsatisfying  
- The brain constantly scans the environment for cues  

## Page 61–80

- Behavior change begins with awareness  
- The **Pointing-and-Calling** method increases conscious awareness of habits  
- The **Habit Scorecard** identifies and evaluates current habits  
- **Implementation intentions** specify when and where habits will occur  
- Clear plans reduce decision fatigue and increase follow-through  
- **Habit stacking** links new habits to existing ones  
- Habits can compound positively or negatively when stacked  

## Page 81–100

- People choose actions based on their current context, not future goals  
- Behavior is heavily influenced by environmental cues  
- Behavior is a function of the person and their environment  
- **Suggestion impulse buying** occurs when visual cues trigger imagined needs  
- Environment design can make good habits obvious and easy  
- Habits are associated with specific locations  
- New environments make habit change easier due to lack of existing cues  
- Disciplined people reduce exposure to temptation  
- It is better to avoid temptation than rely on self-control  
- **Cue-induced wanting** triggers cravings for bad habits  
- Self-control is a short-term and unreliable strategy  

## Page 101–120

- The most effective habits are easy to start and hard to stop  
- Reduce friction for good habits and increase friction for bad ones  
- The **Two-Minute Rule** states that new habits should take less than two minutes  
- Habits should be scaled down to their simplest form  
- Standardize before optimizing  
- Motion creates the illusion of progress, but action produces results  
- Repetition, not perfection, builds mastery  
- Consistency matters more than intensity  
- Small habits establish the foundation for larger behaviors  
- Showing up consistently reinforces identity  
- Supernormal stimulus is a heightened version of reality and it ellicits a stronger response than usual
- The modern world exaggerates our cravings for things such as junk food, fashion, porn, etc
- Habits are a dopamine driven feedback-loop. Dopamine is released not only when you experience it, but also when you anticipate it
- Temptation bundling is linking an action that you want to do with an action that you need to do
- Premack's principle - more probably behaviors will reinforce less probably behaviors
- Humans are herd animals and we imitate behaviors based on three groups: the close, the many, and the powerful
- To understand the group of close, we have to look more into the people that we socialize often such as our loved ones. We soak up the behaviors and qualities of those around us
- To understand the group of many, we have to look how we are unsure of a certain online product we tend to look at other users' reviews about it. Often, we neglect efficient solutions and instead use ineffective ones in order to blend in with the group. The human mind wants to get along with others

## Page 121 - 140
- To understand the group of powerful, we have to acknowledge the tendency of humans to imitate the those of the best in their respective fields and imitate them in order to also stand out because we wanted to be validated by others, showcasing the powerful impact of social influence
- Every behavior has a surface layer craving and an underlying motive
- A craving is a specific manifestation of an underlying motive. It also is the sense that something is missing and is the desire to change your internal state. Providing a reason to act based on the gap from your current state to your desired state
- Desire is the difference of where you are now and where you want to be in the future
- You can learn good habits by associating them with a positive emotion
- We are able to set our mind on things, instead of having to do things, we get to do things
- **Motivation ritual** doing something that you love before you do something that you need to do

## Page 141 - 160
- We tend to focus more on planning than the action itself
- Motion is preparation. The key to building good habits is to not let motion be a form of procastination
- Habit formation is the process wherein you build habits through repetition progressively, becoming automatic
- **Long-term potentiation** is the strengthening of connections between neurons in the brain based on the patterns of recent activities
- **Automaticity** is the ability to perform a behavior without thinking about each steps
- Habits form based on frequency not time
- Our real motivation is to be lazy and do what is convenient
- As mentioned previously, our goals are different from our actions, so habits are an obstacle to what we really want
- **Addition by subtraction** is a method used to reduce friction for a behavior to happen
- Preparing your environment to make habits easier/harder

## Page 161 - 180
- Habits are the entry point, not the end point
- **Two Minute Rule** is to start a behavior for two minutes, it should not be that hard. This keeps a snowball effect once you do. The point is not to do one thing, but to master the habit of showing up since habit must be established before it can be improved
- Make it easy to start and the rest will follow
- Sometimes success is about making bad habits hard
- **Commitment device** is a choice you make in the present that controls the future
- The best way to break a bad habit is to make it impractical to do
- Through the use of technology, we can further make good habits easy and bad habits hard

## Page 181 - 200
- Oftentimes the problem when performing a behavior is not about knowledge but about action and consistency 
- The reward we get from our behaviors are what makes it repeatable. If the reward is satisfying then we will most likely repeat the behavior, if not then we would not
- **Immediate Return Environments** where actions deliver clear and immediate response 
- **Time Inconsistencies** the way our brains evaluate rewards is inconsistent across time
- When the immediate consequences are favorable, the later consequences are disastrous, and vice versa
- Select short term rewards towards your identity

## Page 201 - 220
- The first mistake is not the one that ruins the habit, but the repeated mistakes that follow
- What separates winners from losers is the mindset that if you cannot do it perfectly, then you cannot do it at all
- Avoid the all-or-nothing mindset when it comes to behaviors and habits
- **Goodhart's Law** when a measure becomes the target, it ceases to be a good measure. When we choose the wrong measurement, we optimize the wrong behavior
- When the consequences are severe, people learn quickly
- Align habits with your natural inclination and abilities
- Big five spectrums of behavior are:
    1. Openness to experience
    2. Conscientiousness
    3. Extroversion
    4. Agreeableness
    5. Neuroticism

## Page 221 - 240
- A person's personality allows other habits to become stronger, so choose habits based on the personality of a person
- You must choose habits that are in favor of your interests and needs
- Make sure that there is openness to new experiences when forming new habits to broaden perspective
- Each one of us are capable of doing specific things which makes us unique
- Our genes does not tell us to stop working hard, but what to work hard on
- **Goldilocks Rule** states that human experience peak motivation when working on tasks that are right in the edge
- **Flow State** is the experience of being "in the zone" and fully immersed in an activity
- One must be able to withstand the boredom of habits
- Due to the nature of habits becoming automatic, there will be a decline in performance over time, meaning that we stopped being mindful of our actions
- **Mastery** is the process of narrowing down your focus to a tiny part of success, repeating it over and over again, until it becomes automatic. Essentially, challenging yourself once you get the hang of things

## Page 241 - 260
- Establish a system of reflection and review
- Consistency in effort is important and is a crucial part of success. To learn how to do things right, and to do them the same way
- Improvement is not just learning habits, but also fine-tuning them
- Often our identity develops pride, through this we are unable to see different perspectives and provide more for growth to ourselves. In order to avoid this, we should not make a small aspect of our identity become who we are. When we let a single aspect define us, what happens if that aspect becomes to be vague or stops being? We would have an identity crisis
